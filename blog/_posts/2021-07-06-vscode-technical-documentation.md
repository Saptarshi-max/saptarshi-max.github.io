#  Tips on writing a great technical documentation, and how VSCode can help you enhance your workflow
Weather you are a junior system developer or a technical director, You need to write a documentation to describe your project for your potential clients or new team member to explore the scope of scalability. Technical Documentation is not just about writing down a manual for your work, but a well-structured roadmap for the operator to explore key features your product has to offer, use it for their own benefit and suggest ideas back to the developer for possible improvements of the system and also of course loves to explore the tool you have to offer.

Writing a technical documentation can be a bit overwhelming, As it involves a lot of testing, consistency and even illustrations to make the contents easier to understand for operators of various experience levels. 

## 6 Key Factors to make your Technical Documentation to stand out 

### 1. Make a Layout Plan

Know your audience, Layout sounds pretty basic and straightforward, but it takes quite a lot of planning and brainstorming. A well defined consistent roadmap guides the readers curiosity gradually towards key aspects your tools have to offer. 

So even if the operator posses little knowledge of your tool in general, The layout structure will help in parsing through the contents quickly. 

### 2. Visually appealing 
 When the subject matter is complicated, dense and technical, it's better to explain your ideas through supporting graphics as it helps in breaking the constant monotony of words, while reducing the length of your document it also helps your audience to engage with the contents in a whole new level. 

 A schema is a visual representation of a complex engineering solution. It should be understandable without any other text

### 4. Deadline 
Collecting contents to write a Documentation can be like running in a maze, With so much contents, approaches and possible issues and warnings its easy for anyone to feel overwhelmed.

 It's extremely important to set a Deadline first and then subdivide your plan for the contents in between the days you have between the starting day and the deadline.
